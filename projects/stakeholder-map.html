Stakeholder
Map

An interactive canvas to surface invisible relationships and hidden influence.

The What

Stakeholder Map is a drag-and-drop canvas where you place stakeholders on a two-axis grid — influence vs. interest — and draw relationship lines between them. Each node holds a name, role, communication preference, and a short notes field. The whole thing exports as a clean PNG for decks.

It's deliberately opinionated: the grid forces you to make a judgment call about every person on the list. You can't just add someone without placing them, which is the point.

The Why

I inherited a project midway through and discovered twelve people who thought they were stakeholders, three who were but didn't know it, and one executive who'd been briefed incorrectly for two months. Nobody had a map.

Every stakeholder mapping exercise I'd done used a 2x2 PowerPoint slide that lived in a deck nobody opened again. I wanted something that felt like a live document — something you'd update the week before a steering committee, not build once at kickoff and forget.

What I Learned

  • Drag-and-drop is deceptively complex. Getting smooth, accessible drag-and-drop without a library took far longer than expected. I eventually used a minimal library, but understanding the pointer events model first made me much faster at debugging edge cases.

  • The tool shapes the conversation. When I used this in a real planning session, stakeholders disputed each other's placements. That argument was more valuable than anything written in the notes. A good tool makes disagreement visible and productive.

  • Export fidelity matters more than you think. The first PNG exports looked fine on screen and terrible in a deck. I learned about device pixel ratio, canvas scaling, and why you should always test your output in the context where it will actually be used.

  • Structure is a design decision, not a constraint. The forced two-axis grid was the most controversial feature in early feedback — "too rigid." But every person who used it in a real project said the rigidity was what made it useful. Constraints create clarity.
