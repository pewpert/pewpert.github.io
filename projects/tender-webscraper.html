Automated Tender
Webscraper

Eliminating a daily manual chore — so the team could focus on the deals worth winning.

The What

A Python-based webscraper built for the Business Development team at ST Logistics. It runs on a daily schedule, scans public tender portals for newly posted opportunities, filters them against relevance criteria, and compiles the results into a structured output ready for review.

Before this, a member of the team spent part of every morning manually loading the same pages, cross-referencing entries, and copying data into a spreadsheet. The scraper reduced that to zero — the data is there when they arrive.

The Why

Repetitive data collection is an obvious automation target — but it is surprisingly easy to leave it untouched because "it only takes ten minutes." Over a year, those ten minutes become days. More importantly, it's the kind of task that competes with higher-value thinking during the exact window when focus matters most.

I built this as part of my work in the BD team at ST Logistics (Jan–Apr 2025). The goal was not just efficiency, but reliability: no missed tenders due to a busy morning, no inconsistent filtering depending on who ran the check.

What I Learned

  • Scraper fragility is the real maintenance cost. HTML structures change without warning. I built in lightweight structural checks that alert rather than silently return empty results, so a site change gets caught immediately instead of two weeks later.

  • The output format matters as much as the data. A raw dump is as bad as no automation. I worked with the team to understand how they actually use the shortlist — and structured the output around their existing Excel workflow so adoption was instant.

  • Automation earns trust by being predictable. The team needed to know the tool ran and what it found, even on days with zero relevant tenders. A clear "nothing new today" signal is more useful than silence.
